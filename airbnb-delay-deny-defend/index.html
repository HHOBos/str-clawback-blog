Delay, Deny, Defend

The insurance industry wrote the book on not paying claims. Airbnb perfected the playbook against hosts.

STR ClawBack · March 20, 2026 · 7 min read

In 2010, Jay Feinman published Delay, Deny, Defend — an exposé on how insurance companies systematically avoid paying legitimate claims. The tactics were simple: drag out the process, reject valid claims on technicalities, and fight anyone who pushes back. A decade and a half later, Airbnb is running the same playbook against short-term rental hosts.

If you've ever filed a damage claim, disputed a guest refund, or tried to collect on Airbnb's Host Damage Protection, you've lived this. The language is different — "community support" instead of "claims adjuster" — but the mechanics are identical. And the outcome is the same: you don't get paid.

"The goal is not to deny every claim. The goal is to make the process painful enough that most people stop asking." — The quiet logic behind every support ticket that goes nowhere

Here's how the three pillars of the insurance denial playbook map directly onto Airbnb's approach to host disputes.

• • •
Pillar One

Delay: Run Out the Clock

Insurance companies discovered decades ago that time is their greatest weapon. The longer a claim stays open, the more likely the claimant is to accept a lowball offer — or give up entirely. Airbnb has industrialized this.

File a Host Damage Protection claim and watch what happens. First, you wait 24-72 hours for an initial response. Then an agent asks for documentation you already submitted. You re-upload everything. Another 48-hour wait. A new agent picks up the case — no context, no history — and asks you to start over. The cycle repeats.

Meanwhile, your calendar is blocked, your cash flow is disrupted, and every hour you spend chasing this claim is an hour you're not running your business. For a host managing 20 or 50 properties, multiply that across every dispute and you're looking at a part-time job that pays nothing.

  • The transfer loop: Each new agent resets progress to zero. There's no case continuity by design.
  • The documentation treadmill: Photos aren't clear enough. Receipts need to be itemized differently. The timestamp format isn't right. Every request buys another 48 hours.
  • The silence strategy: Sometimes they simply stop responding. No denial, no resolution — just silence. You're left to decide whether to start over or walk away.
The insurance parallel: Feinman documented insurers who deliberately understaffed claims departments to create backlogs. Airbnb doesn't need to understaff — they just route you through an endless loop of agents with no authority to resolve anything.
Pillar Two

Deny: Find Any Reason to Say No

In the insurance world, denial comes in two flavors: the outright rejection and the technicality. Airbnb has mastered both.

The outright rejection is the blunt instrument. "After careful review, we've determined this claim does not qualify for coverage under our Host Damage Protection program." No explanation of why. No indication of what evidence would change the outcome. Just a form response that reads like it was written before anyone looked at your case.

The technicality is more insidious. You filed your claim 15 hours after checkout instead of 14. The guest's message admitting fault was sent through text, not Airbnb's messaging platform. Your pre-check-in photos were taken two days before the guest arrived, not the day of. The rules shift just enough to exclude you every time.

The most damaging denial tactic is the unilateral guest refund. A guest complains about a "cleanliness issue" — no photos, no specifics — and Airbnb refunds them from your payout before you're even notified. You didn't get to respond. You didn't get to present evidence. The money is already gone, and now the burden is on you to prove you deserve it back.

The insurance parallel: Feinman called this "post-claim underwriting" — finding reasons to deny a claim after it's been filed by scrutinizing the policy for exclusions that were never mentioned at purchase. Airbnb's version: burying the disqualifying rules in a Terms of Service document that changes quarterly.
Pillar Three

Defend: Outlast Anyone Who Fights Back

The final pillar is where the power imbalance becomes undeniable. When a host refuses to accept a denial and escalates, Airbnb doesn't fold — they dig in.

The "appeal" process routes you back to the same team with the same incentives. You're not getting a fresh set of eyes. You're getting the same conclusion delivered by a different person. The resolution center is designed to close cases, not reopen them.

And if you escalate beyond the platform? Airbnb's Terms of Service include a mandatory arbitration clause. You can't take them to court. You can't join a class action. You're forced into a private dispute resolution process where Airbnb has the resources, the lawyers, and the experience — and you have a folder of screenshots and a $3,200 damage claim that isn't worth hiring an attorney over.

That's the point. The economics of defending any individual claim don't make sense for a single host. Airbnb knows this. Insurance companies knew it too. The system is calibrated so that fighting back costs more than giving up.

  • Arbitration as a shield: By forcing disputes into private arbitration, Airbnb avoids public precedent, avoids class actions, and ensures each host fights alone.
  • The volume advantage: Airbnb handles millions of disputes. You handle a handful. They have playbooks, templates, and legal teams optimized for this. You have a spreadsheet and a sense of injustice.
  • Reputation leverage: Hosts who push too hard risk account suspensions, search ranking penalties, or quiet deactivation. The platform controls your distribution — and they know it.
The insurance parallel: Feinman described insurers who would rather spend $50,000 defending a $20,000 claim than pay it — because paying sets a precedent that costs millions. Airbnb's version is more efficient: they just make the process so painful that you never get to the point of fighting.
• • •

Feinman's book changed how regulators looked at the insurance industry. It exposed a system that was rigged against policyholders — not by accident, but by design. The OTA industry hasn't had its reckoning yet. But the tactics are the same, the outcomes are the same, and the hosts absorbing the losses are the same people the platform claims to empower.

The difference between insurance and Airbnb? Insurance companies are regulated. State agencies investigate bad-faith claims practices. There are laws — imperfect, but real — that penalize systematic denial. The OTA space has none of that. Airbnb operates as judge, jury, and executioner in every dispute, with no external oversight and no accountability.
